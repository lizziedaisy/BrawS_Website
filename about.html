BrawStats is software for exploring research design and statistics. It is for everything: learning, teaching, and research.



BrawStats was developed as a graphical teaching tool to illustrate variables, design choices, and null hypothesis testing - and its flaws - and offer students the chance to learn through simulation, observation and experimentation. It has grown into a tool that can be used by learners, educators, and researchers alike to design research and simulate outcomes. It can also import data for analysis and exploration. 

BrawStats encourages critical thinking, and incorporates traditional statistics approaches such as null hypothesis testing alongside new ideas such as effect sizes and confidence intervals. It deliberately teaches research methods as a set of decisions with consequences that are entirely under the control of the researcher. It avoids statistical analysis being perceived as a disconnected, complicated set of facts, rules and rituals. 

BrawStats gives students the power to understand the concepts behind good research from the beginning. They can control and watch every stage of the process graphically. They can make changes to a study and watch the consequences of those changes appear instantly onscreen.

We are creating a new generation of students who hold a deep understanding of what statistical analysis does and therefore what it means.

BrawStats Functions

Functions


Learners


  • Simulate research:
    • Create a hypothesis
    • Add a research design
    • Then simulate data - repeatedly
    • What does the data look like?

  • Go back and change things:
    • Explore design choices
    • What is the consequence of your change?

  • Learn concepts through graphics:
    • Effect sizes
    • Confounds and interactions
    • P-values

  • Import and analyse your own data:
    • Compatible with other major software choices

Researchers


  • Simulate research:
    • Explore advanced design choices
    • Try out abnormal data sets
    • Run power analyses

  • Optimise design:
    • Assess variable and design decisions
    • Sensitivity analysis

  • Import collected data:
    • Visualise data in 3D
    • Explore outliers

Teachers


  • Learn by doing:
    • Which graphs link to which design?
    • What test is used here?

  • Set discovery projects:
    • Does skew or kurtosis matter?
    • Is non-independence a problem?

  • Deliver assessments:
    • Create unique data sets
    • Machine mark answers
    • Check for plagiarism
